HRV — heart rate variability is time of variation between heartbeats in milliseconds. A high HRV means your body is calm and ready for excercise. A low HRV means your body is still dealing with stress, hard training, or poor sleep.

Why it matters: WHOOP gives you a recovery number, but HRV is the significant figure underneath it. Watching the trend over months reveals your fitness habits.

Use the range slider at the bottom to zoom into any stretch of weeks. Hover any dot to see the full breakdown for that day.

Patterns indicate consistencies or inconsistencies in training and recovery. Dips can be seen after harder training blocks or busier times of the year. Spikes can be seen after rest.

WHOOP says sleep drives recovery. But which part of sleep? Total hours? Efficiency? How much deep or REM sleep you got?

Use the dropdown to swap the x-axis between different sleep metrics. A tight upward cluster means that metric predicts your recovery. A scattered blob means it doesn’t.

Common finding: Sleep performance % and sleep debt tend to be stronger predictors than raw hours — it’s not just about quantity.

Each dot is one day. The color still shows your recovery zone — green dots should cluster toward the top right.

Chart 2 · Which sleep metric actually predicts recovery?

Here’s what WHOOP doesn’t tell you directly: your HRV that morning predicts how hard a workout will feel. When HRV is low, your heart beats faster to produce the same output — your “easy” run suddenly feels hard.

What to look for: A downward trend — higher HRV on the x-axis should mean lower average heart rate on the y-axis. If the pattern holds for you, low-HRV days are days to go easy.

Use the activity buttons to filter by sport. Running and Jiu Jitsu are the most revealing — cardio sports where heart rate directly measures effort.
